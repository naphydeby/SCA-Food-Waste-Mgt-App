Hunger in Lagos

Every Lagosian deserves the certainty of their next meal.

Food Salvage is dedicated to rescuing surplus meals and delivering them at no cost to support Lagosians facing food insecurity, ensuring that no meal goes to waste and no one goes hungry

Hunger and food insecurity remain major challenges in Lagos, Nigeria, driven by a combination of economic instability, high inflation, and rising living costs. The situation has worsened in recent years, with food prices skyrocketing due to factors like currency devaluation, fuel subsidy removal, and supply chain disruptions. As a result, many low-income households struggle to afford basic meals, increasing the prevalence of hunger across the city.

Recent data highlights the severity of the crisis, showing that nearly two-thirds of Nigerian households experience food insecurity. A 2023 report revealed that approximately 62.4% of households faced insufficient food intake, a dramatic rise from 37% in 2019. This surge reflects the impact of worsening economic conditions, as families are increasingly forced to cut back on meal portions or skip meals altogether. The soaring cost of food is a major driver of this crisis, leaving many unable to meet their daily nutritional needs.

Malnutrition is a growing concern, especially among children and vulnerable groups. Statistics indicate that approximately 37% of children under five years old are stunted, meaning they suffer from long-term nutritional deficiencies that hinder their physical and cognitive development. Additionally, 7% of children are wasted, indicating acute malnutrition, while 22% are underweight, highlighting the widespread impact of inadequate food access. These figures underscore the urgent need for interventions to improve food distribution and affordability in Lagos.

Our Story in Figures

The estimated daily food cost for an adult in Lagos, ranging from ₦4,000 to ₦8,500, translates to ₦120,000 to ₦255,000 per month. For many Lagosians, specially those with low incomes, these figures present a financial burden, often forcing them to make difficult choices about their diet. The high cost of food relative to income levels affects proper nutrition, meal quality, and overall well-being. The minimum wage in Nigeria is ₦30,000 per month, although many people in Lagos earn slightly more, the average income for many workers in informal jobs or small businesses ranges between ₦50,000 and ₦150,000 per month. When food expenses alone require ₦120,000 to ₦255,000 per adult, it becomes nearly impossible for low-income earners to afford a balanced diet while also covering other essential expenses such as rent, transportation, healthcare, and utilities. For example, a family of four where only one parent earns ₦80,000 per month would struggle to allocate even ₦40,000 for food, which is significantly lower than the estimated minimum monthly food cost of ₦120,000 per adult. As a result, many families are forced to cut down on portion sizes, skip meals, or rely on cheaper, less nutritious foods.
